Pedram

Azadeh Pourzand
Jan 28, 2018 · 7 min read

My mom was only gone for a month or two, but it felt like a lifetime to me. I was seven. Before she left she gave me a little jar filled with a number of beans. She kissed me, and asked that I would throw away one bean at the end of every day. She said that once the beans in the jar would end she would return. I carried the little jar everywhere with me, in school and at home. Some days I would get anxiety over whether I had thrown out a bean earlier that day or not, trying to decide whether I should throw out another one just in case.

The beans ended, and she did not come back. She called and said that I should add more beans to the jar. Disappointed, I asked, “But, how many more?” She laughed and said, “I don’t know sweetheart, a handful or two.” I added a handful of beans and kept up with the routine of throwing away one bean at a time.

Before the beans ended, I got sick, very sick. I lost half of my weight, with my ribs showing from underneath my clothes. I stopped being able to write the alphabet like other kids in first grade. I began to write everything backwards, like a reflection in the mirror. My teacher told me that I was a disappointment, and not as intelligent as she had hoped. She moved me to a bench in the back of the class where “lazy and dumb” kids would sit.

I continued to be quite sick, and scared of needles. I had to get a few shots every day for my illness. I would wrestle with my dad over the needles and he would have to yell at me against his will so that I would sit still for the IVs and the shots.

While slowly recovering, I started to have a different kind of a routine. I began to go out and play in the street every afternoon. I would come home, do my homework quickly, and go to play in the street.

I made a new friend, Pedram, who was much older than me, maybe 15 even. All the kids in our neighborhood were scared of him, but I was not. I told him once that I was dumb. He looked at me and said, “Don’t ever call yourself dumb, little one. You are not dumb! You are smart, I think.” I responded, “No, I think I am dumb. My teacher thinks so, too. I write everything backwards like you would see them in the mirror. Dumb people do that!” He chuckled and said, “No, what is wrong with writing like you see in the mirror? That is actually kind of cool!” He handed me a chalk and said, “Here, write something on the ground”. I took the chalk and wrote “Baba” backwards. “So cool!” Pedram said. Then he tried to copy the way I had written Baba, and said, “You see. You must be smart, because I am very smart and even I cannot write the way like you do. So, you must be smarter than even me.” And, we both laughed.

Pedram would always watch out for me and protect me. When I was spending time with him I would miss my mom less. He would always come through the street on his bike, not holding the handles and biking around like a king. He would let me sit behind his bike and he would give me a ride around the street. This display of support by him was like an endorsement and would increase my status among other kids in the neighborhood.

I wonder where Pedram is now. I bet his children are now older than him then, or maybe he has no children. Maybe he became a gangster or maybe he became an engineer, and left the country as part of brain drain. Maybe he is a political prisoner, or an unemployed man, or maybe he is quite wealthy and in the business of construction for instance.

One day when I was sad, Pedram caught me crying. I told him that my beans in the jar were ending, and that my mom had not returned home yet. I told him that I was scared my mom would never return. He thought about it a bit, gave me a hug, bought me an ice cream and told me that he was going to sort it all out for me. He said that maybe if I play more I would miss my mom less. I nodded.

The next day Pedram came to our street with a big box of rotten tomatoes. The box seemed heavy, and the tomatoes were moldy and smelly. “Why are you carrying rotten tomatoes, Pedram?” I asked him puzzled. He said proudly, “It is for us to play. You have to learn to play, not just sit in a corner, watch other kids and think about your mom. Let’s go, little one!”

He took a rotten tomato and threw it at me. I did not manage to move, and I was not sure why he was trying to hurt me and make my clothes dirty. My shirt and pants got stained as the rotten tomato hit my chest and fell off on the ground. I screamed, “Stop!” He walked towards me with a big smile, handed me a rotten tomato and walked backwards. “Throw it at me. Hit me, little one. Hit me hard! It will make you feel so good! Bodo [Run]!” He said. “But why?” I responded. “Because I dare you to!” he said while laughing.

Before I knew it, Pedram and I were running around and throwing lots of rotten tomatoes at each other and the walls of neighbors in the street. I was laughing and screaming, running around for my life.

Our game was cut short when an old neighbor came out of her house screaming at us, threatening me to tell my father that we made a mess of the street and made the neighbors’ walls dirty. “You kids have no manners!”, she screamed.

I was scared, trembling of embarrassment. I was usually a quiet kid, and not a troublemaker. Pedram took the box, and quietly took off, waving at me. I looked around while the old lady was still yelling. The world had started to spin around my head, as everywhere I would look I could see red tomato stains.

The old lady finally let me go. I knew she would call my father and tell him, and she did. At night when my dad came home, we had to have a “family discussion” about my recent wrong deeds in the street. I used to like him a lot more when my mom was around. Without my mom he was no longer my trusted buddy with whom I would conspire against mom and with whom I would share secrets. He was much more serious and anxious without mom around. I didn’t like him this way.

Dad asked me to go and apologize to the neighbors together with him, and that was how we began our campaign around the neighborhood. We would knock on the door, offer the neighbors sweets, I would keep my head down and apologize while my dad would promise the neighbors that no such thing would ever happen again.

The neighbors collectively pointed out that it was not really my fault, and that this other older boy who did not even belong to this “good” neighborhood is to blame. “Who is he?”, “Doesn’t he have a home of his own?”, “Why does he bike to our street every single day?” ,“He doesn’t even look proper”, “He is dangerous to have around” — the neighbors seemed to have consensus over not liking Pedram. They all blamed him in the end. I thought they were wrong about him. They all called him “badly behaved” and “possibly from a unruly and uneducated family”. I wanted to defend him. But, I had promised my dad to quietly listen, and so I did.

I am not sure what happened to Pedram, or whether someone important in the neighborhood banned him from coming to our street or near me. But, he disappeared. I missed him so much. I wanted him to come by so that I could tell him the beans ended and my mom returned. I wanted to tell him that playing with those rotten tomatoes was a lot of fun, and that I considered him my best friend. I worried for him. But, there was no sign of him. I was also not allowed to go out and play in the street much anymore.

One day as I was sitting in the school bus on my way home from school, I saw Pedram biking on the other side of the street. I took my head out of the window and said with much excitement, “Pedram! Pedram! Salam! Where have you been? Come by today!” The bus driver saw me from his mirror and yelled at me to bring my head back in. Pedram heard me and looked back at me briefly, but then pedaled very fast and left.

That was our last encounter. He never stopped by.

He remains among my most vivid memories of Iran. He sometimes comes to my dream with his heavy box of stolen rotten tomatoes, and every time he manages to convince me to do what we did all over again. Every time that he shows up in my dream we end up throwing rotten tomatoes at each other and at the walls, making a mess out of the whole street. Every time we get in trouble for it, and every time I lose him forever all over again.
