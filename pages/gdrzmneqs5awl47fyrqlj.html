Palos Verdes Homes Association extends board election for the first time

  • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
  • Click to email this to a friend (Opens in new window)
  • Click to print (Opens in new window)
By Valerie Osier | vosier@scng.com | Daily Breeze
PUBLISHED: January 15, 2018 at 5:55 pm | UPDATED: January 15, 2018 at 6:01 pm

For the first time in recent history, the Palos Verdes Homes Association Board of Directors election has decided to extend its election balloting until Feb. 8 in an attempt to finally reach a majority of voters so the results would count.

The board announced at the PVHA annual meeting that a quorum had not been reached for the ninth year in a row, leading to an ongoing lawsuit between the association and an opposition group of residents.

Of 1,438 envelopes received, 1,397 included proper member verification and were considered valid in this year’s election. To reach a quorum, the association needs 2,711 votes of about 5,400 residents. Otherwise, the ballots are not tallied and the current board members maintain their positions.

The homes association predates the cities on the Peninsula and it, along with its Art Jury, is considered a powerful part of the community. It encompasses all of Palos Verdes Estates and the Miraleste portion of Rancho Palos Verdes.

“Hopefully, we’ll have some members who, for whatever reason, weren’t able to vote before be able to vote now,” said Kim Robinson, manager of the PVHA. “… It’s something that were all hopeful that it’ll help us reach a quorum.”

Moss Adams, the accounting firm handling the mailings, will send another ballot marked “urgent” to residents who didn’t participate during the initial voting period.

Retired Superior Court Judge Michael Latin, inspector of elections, will oversee the ballot processing Feb. 9 and will announce the results at a special meeting scheduled for Feb. 13 at 4 p.m. in the Palos Verdes Estates City Council chambers.

Robinson said residents who don’t want to vote can still help the association reach a quorum by returning their ballots with the member verification envelope properly completed. They can even leave the inside ballot blank or crossed out if they really don’t want to vote, she said.

As of now, only the member verification envelopes have been counted. They will be opened and the votes tallied once the association gets enough participants in the election.
